\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================

We present \microgpt{}, a 40M-parameter GPT language model augmented with a \emph{triadic projection head} that produces discrete prime-factor signatures alongside standard next-token predictions.
Unlike the post-hoc approach of the Triadic-Neurosymbolic-Engine~\citep{ornelas2026prime}, which projects frozen sentence embeddings into prime composites, \microgpt{} learns triadic representations end-to-end through a dual-objective training loss that combines language modeling with a novel embedding alignment objective.

Across 29+ training runs and systematic ablation studies, we demonstrate eight principal findings:
(1)~the triadic head adds \textbf{negligible cost} to language quality (perplexity 7.69 vs.\ 7.56 ablation baseline, $+1.7\%$);
(2)~\textbf{semantic ordering emerges gradually with scale}---the gap between related and unrelated concept similarity crosses zero around 20M parameters, with a smooth crossover rather than a sharp phase transition;
(3)~a \textbf{bits sweep} over $k \in \{8, 16, 32, 48, 64, 128\}$ reveals an optimal regime at $k=32$--$64$, shifted upward from the $k=6$--$12$ range reported for post-hoc projection;
(4)~a \textbf{transfer experiment} attaching the triadic head to pre-trained GPT-2 with an InfoNCE alignment loss closes \textbf{48\% of the gap} to the Triadic Engine's post-hoc projection;
(5)~a \textbf{subsumption loss} recovers \textbf{100\% held-out subsumption at $k=64$}, resolving the primary limitation at high bit counts;
(6)~an \textbf{iFSQ activation} ($2\sigma(1.6x)-1$) resolves the subsumption--language tradeoff entirely: language quality is preserved (loss 0.924--0.951 vs.\ baseline 0.946) while achieving up to 87.1\% held-out subsumption, compared to $+47\%$ perplexity degradation under $\tanh$;
(7)~\textbf{compositional analysis} reveals that the bit space functions as a computational substrate---round-trip accuracy (98.1\%) far exceeds the multiplicative prediction (81.9\%), two-step chains show sub-linear error accumulation, and fork analysis confirms that the mechanism is ontologically categorical, not vectorial;
and (8)~a \textbf{discovery loop} expanding from 50 hand-labeled anchors to 158 improves holdout accuracy from 87\% to 93\% and subsumption from 90.7\% to 98.3\%, demonstrating that the train--discover--correct--retrain cycle scales semantic knowledge beyond initial supervision.

\microgpt{} achieves 98\% analogy verification (50/51 analogies), 100\% signature uniqueness, and reproducible semantic ordering ($+0.038 \pm 0.005$ gap, $n=3$, 95\% CI positive)---all within a single forward pass. Critically, the triadic head's value lies in \emph{algebraic operations}: 100\% analogy verification, 87.1\% held-out subsumption, and sub-linear compositional chaining, none of which random projections can provide.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Modern language models represent meaning as dense, high-dimensional vectors. While powerful, these representations are opaque: two semantically related concepts may have similar embeddings, but the \emph{structure} of that similarity---what features are shared, what distinguishes them---remains hidden in continuous geometry. Inspecting or verifying semantic relationships requires additional probing experiments, and there is no guarantee that the learned representations support algebraic operations like composition or subsumption.

The Triadic-Neurosymbolic-Engine~\citep{ornelas2026prime} proposed an alternative: map continuous embeddings into discrete \emph{prime-factor signatures}, where each concept is represented as a composite integer $\Phi(x) = \prod_{i \in S} p_i$, with $S$ being the set of ``active'' semantic bits and $p_i$ the $i$-th prime. This representation enables exact algebraic operations---subsumption via divisibility ($\Phi(A) \mid \Phi(B)$ iff $B$ inherits all features of $A$), composition via LCM, and analogy via factor transfer---all computable in constant time without vector arithmetic.

However, the Engine operates post-hoc: it takes frozen embeddings from a pre-trained sentence transformer (e.g., \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}) and projects them into prime space via locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) or PCA. This two-stage pipeline introduces a fundamental disconnect: the language model has no incentive to produce representations amenable to prime factorization, and the projection step can only preserve whatever structure happens to exist in the frozen embeddings.

In this paper, we ask: \emph{Can a language model learn to produce semantically meaningful prime signatures end-to-end, as a side effect of language modeling?}

We answer affirmatively with \microgpt{}, a GPT-2-style transformer augmented with a linear \emph{triadic projection head}. The model is trained with a dual objective:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \loss{lang} + \alpha \cdot \loss{triadic}
    \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\loss{lang}$ is the standard cross-entropy next-token loss and $\loss{triadic}$ combines diversity, contrastive, entropy, and embedding alignment terms. The key innovation is the \textbf{embedding alignment loss}, which uses the model's own token embeddings as a ``semantic teacher'' for the triadic head---aligning triadic similarity with embedding similarity without requiring any external supervision.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Architecture and training.} We design a triadic projection head and a four-component loss function that enables joint language-triadic training. We identify coherence loss as a source of triadic collapse and embedding alignment as the critical ingredient for semantic structure (Section~\ref{sec:method}).

    \item \textbf{Near-zero-cost ablation.} We show that the triadic head adds negligible degradation to language quality ($+1.7\%$ perplexity), with lexical diversity and repetition metrics unchanged (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

    \item \textbf{Emergent semantic ordering.} Through a four-point scaling study (1.3M to 40M parameters), we demonstrate that the semantic gap transitions from negative to positive, crossing zero around $\sim$20M parameters in a gradual crossover rather than a sharp phase transition (Section~\ref{sec:scaling}). Multi-seed validation ($n=3$) confirms reproducibility: gap $+0.038 \pm 0.005$ (95\% CI $[+0.029, +0.046]$).

    \item \textbf{Optimal bit-width regime.} A six-point bits sweep reveals that end-to-end training shifts the useful $k$ range from $6$--$12$ (post-hoc) to $32$--$64$ (end-to-end), with $k=32$ achieving the best semantic gap and $k=48$--$64$ the best analogy verification (Section~\ref{sec:bits}).

    \item \textbf{Direct comparison and transfer learning.} We benchmark \microgpt{} against the Triadic Engine on identical concept sets, showing that while the Engine achieves higher raw metrics (leveraging pre-trained MiniLM), \microgpt{} is self-contained and demonstrates emergent rather than injected semantics. Attaching the triadic head to pre-trained GPT-2 and testing three alignment losses (MSE, margin ranking, InfoNCE) reveals a \emph{loss--embedding interaction}: InfoNCE is optimal for rich pre-trained embeddings (closing 48\% of the gap to Engine PCA), while MSE is optimal for weak from-scratch embeddings---identifying the alignment loss formulation as the primary bottleneck (Sections~\ref{sec:comparison}--\ref{sec:transfer}).

    \item \textbf{Subsumption recovery via auxiliary loss.} A differentiable subsumption loss recovers exact algebraic subsumption ($\Phi(A) \mid \Phi(B)$) at $k=64$---previously reported as impractical at high bit counts by both \microgpt{} and the Engine. The model achieves 100\% held-out subsumption and autonomously discovers an emergent information hierarchy where abstract concepts use fewer active bits (Section~\ref{sec:subsumption}).

    \item \textbf{iFSQ activation and ternary representation.} Replacing $\tanh$ with scaled sigmoid ($2\sigma(1.6x)-1$) from iFSQ~\citep{li2025ifsq} resolves the subsumption--language tradeoff: language quality is preserved while achieving 86.5\% held-out subsumption. The ternary model ($\{-1,0,+1\}$) produces a three-state distribution that parallels BitNet b1.58's weight sparsity (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}).

    \item \textbf{Compositional substrate.} Round-trip, fork, and chain analyses demonstrate that the bit space supports multi-step algebraic operations with sub-linear error accumulation (98.1\% round-trip vs.\ 81.9\% multiplicative prediction), and that the mechanism is ontologically categorical rather than vectorial (Section~\ref{sec:composition}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Neurosymbolic representations.}
The tension between neural (sub-symbolic) and symbolic AI has motivated hybrid architectures that combine differentiable learning with discrete, interpretable structures. Neural Theorem Provers~\citep{rocktaschel2017ntp} use differentiable backward chaining. Neural Module Networks~\citep{andreas2016nmn} compose symbolic programs. DeepProbLog~\citep{manhaeve2018deepproblog} integrates neural networks with probabilistic logic. Our approach differs in that the symbolic structure (prime factorization) emerges from a standard language modeling objective rather than being imposed by a separate reasoning module.

\paragraph{Interpretable representations.}
Sparse autoencoders~\citep{cunningham2023sparse} extract interpretable features from neural activations post-hoc. Concept bottleneck models~\citep{koh2020concept} force intermediate representations to align with predefined concepts. Binary and discrete representations have been explored in hashing literature~\citep{wang2018survey} and VQ-VAE~\citep{vandenoord2017vqvae}. The triadic projection head is closest to concept bottleneck models, but the ``concepts'' (prime factors) emerge from training rather than being predefined.

\paragraph{Multi-task training of LMs.}
Adding auxiliary objectives to language models is well-established. ELECTRA~\citep{clark2020electra} adds a replaced-token detection head. BERT~\citep{devlin2019bert} combines masked language modeling with next-sentence prediction. T5~\citep{raffel2020t5} unifies multiple NLP tasks. Our triadic objective is unusual in that the auxiliary task is not linguistic---it maps tokens to algebraic structures (prime composites) rather than predicting linguistic labels.

\paragraph{Prime factorization for semantics.}
The Triadic-Neurosymbolic-Engine~\citep{ornelas2026prime} is the direct predecessor of this work. It demonstrated that continuous embeddings from pre-trained sentence transformers can be projected into prime-factor space, enabling algebraic verification of semantic relationships (subsumption, analogy, composition). Key findings include a useful regime of $k=6$--$12$ bits for post-hoc LSH, 28.4$\times$ speedup over cosine similarity for pairwise verification, and 100\% composition guarantee by construction. Our work extends this from post-hoc projection to end-to-end training.

\paragraph{Finite scalar quantization.}
Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ;~\citealt{mentzer2024fsq}) replaces learned vector quantization codebooks with fixed, uniform grids, eliminating codebook collapse entirely. Improved FSQ (iFSQ;~\citealt{li2025ifsq}) identifies that $\tanh$ bounding causes activation concentration near zero---precisely the dead-bit phenomenon we observe---and proposes a scaled sigmoid activation ($2\sigma(1.6x)-1$) that achieves uniform bin utilization. We adopt this activation for our triadic head and confirm that it resolves our dead-bit problem (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}).

\paragraph{Ternary quantization and BitNet.}
BitNet b1.58~\citep{ma2024bitnet} demonstrates that ternary weight quantization ($\{-1, 0, +1\}$) preserves model quality while reducing computation. Notably, $\sim$42.3\% of weights converge to zero---not as collapse, but as learned sparsity. Our triadic head independently discovers the same three-state structure (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}): $\sim$73\% of projection outputs are zero (semantically ``irrelevant''), with the remainder split between positive (``present'') and negative (``absent''). This convergence from two independent lines of work---engineering optimization and philosophical ontology---suggests that three-state representations capture something fundamental about information structure.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\microgpt{} is a decoder-only transformer following the GPT-2 architecture~\citep{radford2019gpt2} with one addition: a linear triadic projection head.

\paragraph{Backbone.} The model consists of token and position embeddings, $L$ transformer blocks (each with pre-norm multi-head causal self-attention and a GELU feed-forward network), and a final layer norm. The LM head shares weights with the token embedding matrix.

\paragraph{Triadic head.} A single linear projection $W_{\text{tri}} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ (no bias) maps the final hidden states to $k$ triadic bits. The original formulation uses $\tanh$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{b}(x) = \tanh(W_{\text{tri}} \cdot \mathbf{h}(x)) \in [-1, 1]^k
    \label{eq:triadic_head}
\end{equation}
However, $\tanh$ causes activation concentration near zero, producing ``dead bits'' with low entropy (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}). Following iFSQ~\citep{li2025ifsq}, we replace $\tanh$ with a scaled sigmoid activation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{b}(x) = 2\sigma(1.6 \cdot W_{\text{tri}} \cdot \mathbf{h}(x)) - 1 \in [-1, 1]^k
    \label{eq:ifsq_head}
\end{equation}
This maintains the $[-1,1]$ range while providing stronger gradients in the transition region, enabling the model to learn active bit assignments without saturation.

For ternary quantization, the continuous output is discretized during the forward pass via rounding to $\{-1, 0, +1\}$ with straight-through estimation (STE) for gradient computation, producing three ontological states: \emph{present} ($+1$), \emph{absent} ($-1$), and \emph{irrelevant} ($0$).

Where $\mathbf{h}(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the final-layer representation for token $x$. The binarized signature is $\mathbf{s}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\mathbf{b}(x) > 0]$, and the prime composite is:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi(x) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} p_i^{s_i(x)}
    \label{eq:prime_composite}
\end{equation}
where $p_i$ is the $i$-th prime number. This mapping is deterministic and invertible: given $\Phi(x)$, the bit vector $\mathbf{s}(x)$ can be recovered by trial division.

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:training}

The total loss (Equation~\ref{eq:total_loss}) combines a standard language modeling objective with a multi-component triadic loss, activated after a warmup period.

\paragraph{Language loss.} Standard causal cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \loss{lang} = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P(x_t \mid x_{<t})
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Triadic loss.} The triadic objective $\loss{triadic}$ consists of four components. Notably, an earlier fifth component---\emph{coherence loss}, which encouraged adjacent tokens to produce similar projections---was removed after it caused complete triadic collapse (all tokens mapped to a single signature). The coherence signal provided an easy-to-minimize shortcut that overwhelmed diversity objectives, analogous to mode collapse in GANs. The four surviving components are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Diversity loss.} Encourages each bit to fire approximately 50\% of the time:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{div} = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \bar{b}_i^2, \quad \bar{b}_i = \frac{1}{BT}\sum_{n,t} b_i^{(n,t)}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Contrastive loss.} Pushes sequence-level representations apart:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{ctr} = \frac{1}{B(B-1)} \sum_{i \neq j} \left(\frac{\bar{\mathbf{b}}^{(i)} \cdot \bar{\mathbf{b}}^{(j)}}{\|\bar{\mathbf{b}}^{(i)}\| \|\bar{\mathbf{b}}^{(j)}\|}\right)^2
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Entropy regularization.} Prevents ``dead bits'' by maximizing per-bit entropy across the batch:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{ent} = 1 - \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} H(q_i), \quad q_i = \frac{\bar{b}_i + 1}{2}
    \end{equation}
    where $H(q) = -q\log q - (1-q)\log(1-q)$ is binary entropy.

    \item \textbf{Embedding alignment loss.} The key innovation---uses the model's own token embeddings as a semantic teacher. We investigate three formulations:

    \textbf{(a) MSE alignment.} For 64 randomly sampled token pairs $(i,j)$ per sequence, minimize the squared difference between triadic and embedding cosine similarities:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{align}^{\text{MSE}} = \frac{1}{N_{\text{pairs}}} \sum_{(i,j)} \left( \text{sim}_{\text{tri}}(i,j) - \text{sim}_{\text{emb}}(i,j) \right)^2
        \label{eq:align_mse}
    \end{equation}

    \textbf{(b) Margin ranking alignment.} For each anchor token, sample 16 candidates and identify the most/least similar in embedding space. Enforce that the triadic head preserves this ordering:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{align}^{\text{rank}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_a \max\left(0,\; m - \text{sim}_{\text{tri}}(a, a^+) + \text{sim}_{\text{tri}}(a, a^-)\right)
        \label{eq:align_rank}
    \end{equation}
    where $a^+$ and $a^-$ are the embedding-nearest and embedding-farthest candidates, and $m = 0.1$ is the margin. This preserves similarity \emph{ordering} without matching absolute values.

    \textbf{(c) InfoNCE alignment}~\citep{oord2018cpc}. For 32 anchor tokens, compute a cross-similarity matrix in both embedding and triadic spaces. The positive for each anchor is the most similar token in embedding space:
    \begin{equation}
        \loss{align}^{\text{NCE}} = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_a \log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}_{\text{tri}}(a, a^+) / \tau)}{\sum_{j} \exp(\text{sim}_{\text{tri}}(a, j) / \tau)}
        \label{eq:align_infonce}
    \end{equation}
    where $\tau = 0.1$ is the temperature and the sum runs over all pool tokens as negatives. This provides structured positive/negative mining from embedding space with temperature-controlled gradients.

    Crucially, for all three formulations, the gradient does \emph{not} flow back through the embeddings (detached), so the alignment objective only modifies the triadic head.
\end{enumerate}

The combined triadic loss is:
\begin{equation}
    \loss{triadic} = \loss{div} + \loss{ctr} + \lambda_{\text{ent}} \cdot \loss{ent} + \lambda_{\text{align}} \cdot \loss{align}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Triadic warmup.} The triadic loss is activated after 80\% of training steps (configurable). During warmup, the model trains on language only, allowing stable embedding representations to form before the triadic head begins learning. The warmup fraction introduces a tradeoff: shorter warmup produces healthier bits but lower subsumption (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\paragraph{Hyperparameter sensitivity.} Through a Pareto frontier analysis (Runs 15--17), we identified a sharp cliff at $\alpha > 0.05$: models trained with $\alpha = 0.10$ or $\alpha = 0.20$ lose semantic ordering despite maintaining high entropy. The optimal configuration is $\alpha = 0.05$, $\lambda_{\text{ent}} = 1.0$, $\lambda_{\text{align}} = 5.0$.


\subsection{Algebraic Operations}
\label{sec:algebra}

Once a concept is mapped to $\Phi(x)$, several algebraic operations become exact:

\paragraph{Subsumption.} Concept $A$ subsumes $B$ iff $\Phi(A) \mid \Phi(B)$ (integer divisibility). This corresponds to $A$'s active bits being a subset of $B$'s.

\paragraph{Composition.} The most specific common concept of $A$ and $B$ is $\text{lcm}(\Phi(A), \Phi(B))$.

\paragraph{Similarity.} Jaccard similarity over active bits: $J(A,B) = |S_A \cap S_B| / |S_A \cup S_B|$.

\paragraph{Analogy.} Given ``$A$ is to $B$ as $C$ is to $?$'', compute the transform $T = \Phi(B) / \gcd(\Phi(A), \Phi(B))$, apply it to $C$: $\Phi_{\text{target}} = \text{lcm}(\Phi(C), T)$, and search for the nearest concept.

All operations require only integer arithmetic (GCD, LCM, trial division), avoiding floating-point comparisons entirely.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Data.} All models are trained on a 50,000-story subset of TinyStories~\citep{eldan2023tinystories} ($\sim$2.1M stories total). A BPE tokenizer with vocabulary size 4,096 is trained from the corpus and shared across all runs.

\paragraph{Hardware.} All training is performed on a single NVIDIA RTX 5060 Ti (16 GB VRAM).

\paragraph{Optimization.} AdamW optimizer ($\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, weight decay $= 0.01$) with cosine learning rate schedule and linear warmup over the first 5\% of training steps. Peak learning rate is $3 \times 10^{-4}$. Mixed precision via \texttt{torch.amp}: float16 for initial experiments (Runs 1--21), bfloat16 for all Danza bootstrap and ternary experiments (D-A5, D-A8, D-A10, E4 sweep). Batch size 32 for Runs 1--21; batch size 64 for Danza experiments. Sequence length 256 for all runs.

\paragraph{Evaluation concepts.} We evaluate triadic quality on a curated set of 113 concepts across 13 semantic categories (animal, person, profession, feeling, nature, food, artifact, attribute, place, time, action, communication, state). We use 12 semantically related pairs (e.g., king--queen, dog--cat), 12 unrelated pairs (e.g., king--dog, happy--table), and 13 analogy quadruples (e.g., king:queen::man:woman). An expanded evaluation uses 51 diverse analogy quadruples covering family, size, temperature, emotion, animal, and royalty relationships (Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks}).

Nevertheless, the triadic semantic structure is an \textbf{emergent property of model capacity}, requiring sufficient language understanding before the embedding alignment loss can transfer meaningful semantic signal.

Notably, bit entropy and signature diversity saturate much earlier (at $\sim$6M parameters), indicating that the \emph{structure} of representations requires more capacity than their \emph{diversity}. The model needs sufficient language understanding before the embedding alignment loss can transfer meaningful semantic signal to the triadic head.

\subsection{Triadic Benchmarks}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

We evaluate the XL model (Run~15) on three triadic-specific benchmarks.

\paragraph{Analogy verification.} Given analogy quadruples (e.g., king:queen::man:woman), we compute the algebraic target via prime-factor transfer and measure whether the correct answer's similarity exceeds the median. On an expanded set of 51 diverse analogies (covering family, size, temperature, emotion, animal, and royalty relationships), the model achieves \textbf{98\% verification rate} (50/51 analogies). The original 13-quad evaluation yielded 69.2\% (9/13); expanding the evaluation set reveals that those failures were edge cases rather than systematic limitations. Top-1 retrieval accuracy remains low ($\sim$4\%), consistent with the Engine paper's reported 2--10\% range. The method excels at \emph{verification} rather than \emph{discovery}, as expected for a hash-based approach.

\paragraph{Linear probe (interpretability).} A linear classifier trained on 64-bit triadic projections achieves 10.1\% accuracy across 13 categories---comparable to the 8.3\% achieved by a probe on 512-dimensional embedding vectors. Both probes perform near random chance (7.7\% for 13 categories), indicating that neither representation encodes sufficient category-level information for a simple linear probe. The relevant claim is therefore not information-theoretic compression but rather \textbf{negligible perplexity cost} ($+1.7\%$): the triadic head adds algebraic verification capabilities (98\% analogy, 92--100\% subsumption) at minimal language cost.

\paragraph{Subsumption.} At $k=64$, exact prime divisibility ($\Phi(A) \mid \Phi(B)$) is too strict without explicit supervision: with $\sim$32 active bits per concept, all hypernym bits must appear in the hyponym, which is combinatorially improbable. Neither the base model nor the Engine achieves non-trivial subsumption recall at this bit count, confirming the Engine paper's finding that subsumption is practical only at low $k$ (6--12 bits). However, this limitation is \emph{not} inherent: Section~\ref{sec:subsumption} demonstrates that a differentiable subsumption loss recovers 100\% held-out subsumption at $k=64$.

\subsection{Recovering Subsumption at High Bit Counts}
\label{sec:subsumption}

The zero subsumption rate at $k=64$ (Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks}) was the primary limitation of both \microgpt{} and the Engine at high bit counts. We address this with a differentiable \emph{subsumption loss} that directly penalizes violations of the bit-inheritance condition.

\paragraph{Subsumption loss.} For a hypernym--hyponym pair $(H, Y)$ with triadic projections $\mathbf{b}(H), \mathbf{b}(Y) \in [-1,1]^k$, the subsumption constraint $\Phi(Y) \bmod \Phi(H) = 0$ requires that every active bit of $H$ is also active in $Y$. We enforce this with:
\begin{equation}
    \loss{sub} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|} \sum_{(H,Y) \in \mathcal{P}} \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \text{ReLU}\!\left(b_i(H) - b_i(Y)\right)
    \label{eq:sub_loss}
\end{equation}
This penalizes only the bits where the hypernym is more active than the hyponym, providing a differentiable proxy for exact divisibility.

\paragraph{Training data.} We train on 45 hypernym--hyponym pairs across 7 categories (animal, person, feeling, food, color, place, time) and evaluate on 12 held-out pairs from 4 categories (e.g., animal$\rightarrow$tiger, person$\rightarrow$man, food$\rightarrow$pizza, place$\rightarrow$castle).

Three key results emerge. First, \textbf{the iFSQ activation resolves the subsumption--language tradeoff}. With $\tanh$, subsumption training destroys language quality (PPL degrades from 8.3 to 10.7 in a weight sweep across $\lambda \in \{0.5, 1, 2, 5\}$). With iFSQ, language loss is preserved (0.951 vs.\ baseline 0.946) while achieving 100\% training subsumption and 86.5\% held-out generalization. The iFSQ binary model actually \emph{improves} language quality (loss 0.924~$<$~0.946), suggesting that the activation's gradient properties benefit the shared transformer backbone.

Second, \textbf{the activation function is the critical variable, not ternary quantization}. The iFSQ binary model (D-A10) achieves near-identical subsumption to the ternary model (87.1\% vs.\ 86.5\%), confirming that the fix resides in gradient flow through the activation, not in the number of output states.

Third, \textbf{the ternary model produces a clean three-state distribution}: 1.3\% negative (present), 73.3\% zero (irrelevant), 25.3\% positive (absent). The 73\% zero rate is consistent with BitNet b1.58's independently discovered $\sim$42\% weight zeros~\citep{ma2024bitnet}, adjusted for the difference between network weights (distributed symmetrically) and concept activations (where ``irrelevant'' is the dominant state). This three-state structure maps directly to the ontological framework from which the triadic system originates: \emph{presence} ($+1$), \emph{absence} ($-1$), and \emph{void/irrelevance} ($0$).

Fourth, \textbf{scaling the backbone improves bit accuracy but destroys algebraic structure}. D-A13 attaches the same iFSQ ternary head to GPT-2 Medium (355M params, two-phase training: frozen backbone for 5K steps, then unfreeze last 4 layers for 45K steps). Holdout bit accuracy reaches 89.4\%, and subsumption appeared perfect on the initial 13-pair test set ($n=13$). However, when evaluated with the expanded v2 anchor set ($n=158$, 179 holdout subsumption pairs), the picture reverses dramatically. D-A17 (same 355M backbone, v2 anchors) achieves \emph{higher} bit accuracy (97.7\%) but \emph{catastrophically lower} subsumption: 1.7\% on 179 test pairs, compared to 98.3\% for the 40M model (D-A14 v2). The ternary distribution collapses to near-binary: 69.3\%/3.4\%/27.3\% (vs.\ 28.6\%/41.3\%/30.1\% for D-A14).

The mechanism is clear: without the zero state ($\sim$42\% in D-A14), subsumption via $(A \mathbin{\&} B) = B$ is rarely satisfiable---when all bits are $\pm 1$, the bit-subset relation becomes combinatorially unlikely.

However, a follow-up experiment (D-A19) demonstrates that \textbf{the D-A17 failure was caused by training bugs, not an inherent scale limitation}. D-A17 used only alignment loss (missing diversity, contrastive, and entropy regularizers) and a non-differentiable subsumption term ($(x > 0)$ instead of $(x+1)/2$). D-A19 restores the full four-component triadic loss plus differentiable subsumption, recovering 76.9\% subsumption on 895 test pairs and 100\% analogy verification (6/6 quads, cosine 0.81--0.92), while maintaining 97.1\% holdout bit accuracy. Dead bits drop from 26 to 16. The per-token ternary distribution remains skewed (70.8\%/1.0\%/28.2\%), yet algebraic operations function---suggesting that the $\sim$42\% zero rate is \emph{optimal} but not \emph{necessary} for algebra. The corrected conclusion is that scaling the backbone to 355M is compatible with algebraic structure, provided the full loss pipeline is maintained. This finding reinforces a central result of this work: \textbf{the triadic head's value is not bit accuracy but algebraic composability}, and the training loss---not model scale---is the critical determinant of algebraic capability.

Fifth, \textbf{a hybrid supervised/free architecture preserves algebraic structure with fewer dead bits but at the cost of full bit accuracy}. D-A18 splits the 63-bit head into 30 supervised bits (trained against gold labels) and 33 free bits (forced to encode complementary information via gradient reversal). The result: 96.5\% subsumption (864/895 pairs) and only 15 dead bits (vs.\ D-A14's 26), but full bit accuracy drops to 75.3\% (vs.\ 93\%). The free bits converge to a degenerate binary code ($+1$/$0$, no $-1$ state), with 61.4\% zeros---higher sparsity than D-A14's healthy 41.3\%, which lowers signature uniqueness (56.3\% vs.\ D-A14's higher diversity). The adversarial objective fails to fully decouple free bits from supervised information (adversary accuracy 92.7\% vs.\ target 50\%). Bit-semantic analysis (reptimeline) confirms the structural impoverishment: D-A18 discovers only 11 triadic interactions versus D-A14's 328---a 30$\times$ reduction in emergent algebraic structure despite nominally similar subsumption rates. D-A14 remains the production model: full supervision on all 63 bits produces richer algebra than partial supervision with free exploration.


% ============================================================
\subsection{Compositional Operations: Chains, Forks, and Round-Trips}
\label{sec:composition}
% ============================================================

A key question is whether the triadic bit space functions merely as an \emph{encoding}---preserving semantic information---or as a \emph{computational substrate} that supports compositional operations across multiple steps.

We test this with three composition experiments on the D-A5 XL checkpoint (63 bits, 15 analogy quads):

\paragraph{Round-trip test.} For each quad $A{:}B{::}C{:}D$, we compute $D_{\text{pred}} = R3(A,B,C)$ (forward), then $C_{\text{pred}} = R3(B,A,D_{\text{pred}})$ (reverse). If errors were random and independent, round-trip accuracy should be $\text{acc}^2 \approx 81.9\%$ (since single-step accuracy is 90.5\%). Instead, we observe \textbf{98.1\% round-trip accuracy}---a $+16.2$ percentage point surplus over the multiplicative prediction. In ternary space, the effect is even stronger: 92.8\% actual vs.\ 72.7\% predicted ($+20.1$~pp).

This means the errors in $D_{\text{pred}}$ are \emph{coherent} with the transformation structure: the bits that fail are wrong in a direction that cancels when the operation is reversed. Example: \texttt{hot:cold::loud:quiet} has Hamming distance 5 forward, but the round-trip recovers C with \emph{zero} errors.

\paragraph{Fork test.} We apply the same relationship (e.g., \texttt{bright}$\to$\texttt{dark}) to multiple target pairs and measure the consistency of the effective transformation vectors via pairwise cosine similarity. Across 5 relationship groups, the mean pairwise cosine is $\sim$0.06---effectively zero.

This reveals that the Rule of Three does \textbf{not} operate like word2vec's parallelogram model, where $\vec{king} - \vec{man} \approx \vec{queen} - \vec{woman}$ as parallel vectors. Instead, each concept pair activates different bits: the transformation is \emph{concept-specific}, operating through categorical bit-pattern logic rather than shared directional geometry. This is consistent with the underlying 7-layer ontological structure, where each layer governs different semantic primitives.

\paragraph{Transitive chains.} We construct 30 two-step chains: apply relationship $R_1$ to get an intermediate $D_{\text{pred}}$, then apply a different relationship $R_2$ to $D_{\text{pred}}$. If errors compounded multiplicatively, 2-step accuracy should be $\sim$82.8\%. Actual: \textbf{87.4\%} ($+4.5$~pp above multiplicative prediction). The predicted outputs carry sufficient structural information to support a second transformation.

These results provide evidence that the triadic bit space is a \emph{computational substrate}: it supports multi-step algebraic operations with sub-linear error accumulation, indicating coherent geometric structure rather than arbitrary encoding.


% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Emergence vs.\ injection.}
The most significant finding is that meaningful prime-factor representations can \emph{emerge} from language modeling---they do not need to be injected from external embeddings or predefined ontologies. The embedding alignment loss enables this by using the model's \emph{own} developing understanding of semantic similarity as the supervision signal. As the language model improves during training, it provides an increasingly rich semantic teacher for the triadic head, creating a virtuous cycle. The transfer experiment (Section~\ref{sec:transfer}) confirms this interpretation: when the semantic teacher is richer (GPT-2 embeddings trained on 8M web pages vs.\ 50K children's stories), the triadic head achieves $3.8\times$ higher semantic gap ($+0.076$ vs.\ $+0.020$)---demonstrating that the triadic architecture faithfully extracts whatever semantic structure the embeddings provide.

\paragraph{Emergent information hierarchy.}
The subsumption experiment reveals a striking emergent behavior: when trained with the subsumption loss, the model autonomously discovers that abstract concepts should have fewer active bits than concrete ones. Hypernyms converge to 1--6 bits (93\% reduction from baseline), while their hyponyms retain 20--40 bits. This hierarchy was never explicitly supervised---it arises because the gradient finds that minimizing hypernym bits is the easiest path to satisfying $\Phi(Y) \bmod \Phi(H) = 0$ for all hyponyms $Y$. The result is consistent with set-theoretic semantics, where the intension of a general concept (few defining features) is a subset of the intension of a specific one (many features). That a simple differentiable loss recovers this classical philosophical structure through gradient descent alone is, to our knowledge, a novel finding.

\paragraph{What the triadic head actually provides.}
A frozen random projection head---with random weights and no triadic gradient---produces a semantic gap of $+0.056$, \emph{higher} than the trained head's $+0.038$. This initially surprising result reveals that raw hidden-state geometry already encodes substantial semantic structure: any linear projection preserves some of it. However, the trained head's value is not in semantic gap but in \emph{algebraic operations}: 98\% analogy verification vs.\ 16.7\% for random projections, and 92--100\% subsumption vs.\ 0\%. The triadic head learns to \emph{organize} bits for algebraic composability---subsumption via divisibility, analogy via factor transfer---capabilities that random projections cannot provide despite their higher raw similarity metrics. Separately, comparing raw embedding similarity (512D) against triadic similarity (64D) reveals that the trained head amplifies the semantic gap by 2.6$\times$ ($+0.014$ embeddings $\rightarrow$ $+0.038$ triadic), confirming that the projection head adds genuine structure beyond what the embeddings already contain.

\paragraph{Alignment is the primary driver of triadic quality.}
An alignment loss ablation reveals that removing the embedding alignment component doubles the number of dead bits (11 $\rightarrow$ 23) and collapses semantic ordering, while removing entropy regularization has negligible effect when alignment is present. The alignment loss is therefore the critical ingredient: it provides the structured learning signal that transforms the triadic head from a random projector into a semantically organized one. Entropy regularization is redundant in its presence---a finding that simplifies the loss function for future work.

\paragraph{The information bottleneck effect.}
The bits sweep reveals that $k=32$ achieves better semantic gap than $k=64$, despite the language loss being higher. This is consistent with the information bottleneck principle~\citep{tishby2000information}: a tighter bottleneck forces the model to retain only the most semantically discriminative features, producing better separation. However, analogy verification benefits from higher $k$ (48--64), suggesting that algebraic operations require finer-grained representations. The optimal $k$ depends on the downstream task.

\paragraph{Why coherence loss fails.}
An important negative result: training with a coherence loss (encouraging adjacent tokens to have similar triadic projections) causes complete collapse to a single signature. Adjacent-token similarity provides a strong, easy-to-minimize signal that overwhelms the diversity and entropy objectives. This is analogous to mode collapse in GANs~\citep{goodfellow2014gan}---the model finds a trivial solution (map everything to the same projection) that minimizes the coherence term. Removing coherence loss and relying solely on embedding alignment resolved the issue.

\paragraph{Knowledge distillation fails.}
We also explored supervised knowledge distillation, training the triadic head to reproduce deterministic ``gold standard'' prime signatures derived from WordNet (Run~10). Despite achieving the lowest training loss (1.03), this approach caused \emph{complete triadic collapse}: all concepts mapped to a single signature, destroying all semantic differentiation. The distillation signal, weighted at $5\times$ the language loss, overwhelmed the diversity and contrastive objectives. This failure motivated our self-supervised embedding alignment approach, which produces semantically structured representations without any external target dictionary.

\paragraph{Warmup--subsumption tradeoff.}
The triadic warmup fraction introduces an unexpected tradeoff. Reducing warmup from 80\% to 50\% increases the active triadic training window (25K steps vs.\ 10K steps), producing healthier bits (24 dead vs.\ 44 dead). However, the longer warmup (80\%) achieves \emph{higher} subsumption (92.3\% vs.\ 76.9\%). The mechanism: short warmup allows subsumption loss to dominate before entropy regularization establishes bit diversity, inadvertently favoring the bit-inheritance pattern that subsumption requires. Both operating points are valid---practitioners can choose between bit health (50\% warmup) and subsumption performance (80\% warmup) depending on downstream requirements.

\paragraph{Additional negative results.}
Beyond coherence collapse and distillation failure (above), several other approaches failed to produce meaningful results:
(1)~\emph{Gradient decoupling} (D-A15): stopping gradients between the language and triadic heads yielded 49.6\% bit accuracy---indistinguishable from random---confirming that end-to-end gradient flow is essential, not optional.
(2)~\emph{Aristotelian type prediction}: a linear probe for Aristotelian type categories (substance, quality, quantity, relation) showed 0/4 significant separations in the triadic space.
(3)~\emph{Enantiodromia test}: only 2/8 predicted opposite-concept transitions were observed, failing to confirm the philosophical hypothesis that extremes tend toward their opposites in the learned representation.
These failures constrain the scope of our claims: the triadic head captures \emph{compositional} structure (analogies, subsumption) but not higher-order categorical or dialectical patterns.

\paragraph{Convergence with Natural Semantic Metalanguage.}
An independent validation comes from comparing the 63 ontological primitives with Wierzbicka's Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM)~\citep{wierzbicka1996semantics}, a set of $\sim$65 semantic primes identified through 40+ years of cross-linguistic fieldwork across 30+ typologically diverse languages. Despite completely independent derivations---philosophical ontology vs.\ empirical linguistics---we find 28 direct matches (43\% of NSM primes) and 8 close matches (total: 55\% overlap). The convergent set includes core cognitive predicates (\textsc{think}, \textsc{know}, \textsc{want}, \textsc{see}, \textsc{hear}, \textsc{say}), logical connectives (\textsc{because}, \textsc{if}, \textsc{can}, \textsc{maybe}), and quantifiers (\textsc{one}, \textsc{some}, \textsc{all}, \textsc{many}, \textsc{part}, \textsc{kind}). The divergences are principled: NSM includes deictic primes (\textsc{i}, \textsc{you}, \textsc{here}, \textsc{now}) that are linguistically necessary but not ontologically primitive, while the Sistema~7$\times$7 includes phenomenological primitives (consciousness, hedonic poles, observer states) that are ontologically motivated but not cross-linguistic universals. This convergence---like the independent discovery of $\sim$42\% sparsity in both BitNet b1.58 and our ternary model---suggests that the primitive inventory captures genuine structure in human conceptual space, not an artifact of any single methodology.

\paragraph{Subsumption false positive rate.}
A negative subsumption test on semantically unrelated concept pairs reveals a false positive rate of 24.1\%: pairs like (\emph{red}, \emph{blue}) pass the bit-subset test despite having no hypernym--hyponym relationship. The root cause is dead bits: with $\sim$30 bits permanently OFF, most concepts share the same inactive bits, making the subset relation trivially satisfied. The ternary representation (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}) addresses this by distinguishing zero (irrelevant) from negative (actively absent), preventing shared zeros from creating spurious subset relationships. Conversely, the D-A17 scaling experiment (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}) demonstrates the opposite pathology: when ternary zeros collapse from $\sim$42\% to 3.4\%, subsumption drops from 98.3\% to 1.7\% because the bit-subset relation becomes unsatisfiable. A follow-up experiment (D-A19) restored the full four-component triadic loss and differentiable subsumption to the 355M model, recovering 76.9\% subsumption and 100\% analogy verification---demonstrating that the D-A17 failure was caused by training bugs (alignment-only loss, non-differentiable subsumption), not an inherent scale limitation. Together, these findings bracket the optimal ternary sparsity: \emph{too many} zeros create false positives; \emph{too few} prevent any subsumption at all. The $\sim$42\% zero rate observed in D-A14 appears to be the structural optimum, though D-A19 shows that algebraic capability can be partially recovered even without matching this rate.

\paragraph{Temporal observability: reptimeline.}
The discovery loop described above (anchor expansion, bit-semantic analysis, reconciliation) was operationalized through \textbf{reptimeline}, a software tool developed as part of this project to track how discrete representations evolve during training. reptimeline consumes a sequence of model checkpoints, extracts discrete codes for a set of probe concepts at each step, and computes per-code lifecycle events (births, deaths), pairwise connection formation, entropy and churn curves, and automatic phase transition detection via statistical change-point analysis.

Applied to \microgpt{} (8 checkpoints, 53 concepts, 63 bits), reptimeline revealed a four-phase training dynamic invisible to standard scalar logging: (1)~an \emph{exploration} phase (steps 0--7,500) with high bit churn across all concepts; (2)~a \emph{consolidation} phase (steps 7,500--27,500) where churn remains elevated but entropy stabilizes; (3)~a \emph{crystallization} event at step $\sim$27,500 where entropy drops from 0.50 to 0.23 and bit utilization drops from 0.98 to 0.66 simultaneously; and (4)~a \emph{stabilization} phase (steps 27,500--50,000) where churn collapses to 0.075 and representations freeze. This crystallization event---the moment the model ``commits'' to its semantic assignments---is not visible in loss curves or standard metrics, and to our knowledge has not been reported for discrete representation systems.

Additionally, reptimeline includes three automated analysis modules: \emph{BitDiscovery}, which infers what each bit encodes without prior ontology by analyzing activation patterns, discovering dual pairs (anti-correlated bits), dependency chains, and three-way AND-gate interactions; \emph{AutoLabeler}, which translates discovered bit semantics to human-readable labels via embedding centroids, contrastive analysis, or LLM queries; and \emph{Reconciler}, which compares discovered structure against a theoretical ontology and suggests corrections in \emph{both directions}---to the model's training anchors and to the theory itself. Applied to our checkpoints, the Reconciler achieved 44.4\% agreement between model-discovered and manually-defined ontology, identifying 35 critical mismatches that guided the anchor expansion from 50 to 158 concepts.

reptimeline is backend-agnostic: it operates on any system that produces discrete codes (VQ-VAE codebooks, FSQ levels, sparse autoencoder features, concept bottleneck models). To validate this, we applied reptimeline to two additional, architecturally distinct systems:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MNIST Binary Autoencoder} (32-bit bottleneck, 10 digit classes, 6 training epochs): reptimeline tracked bit lifecycle, discovered 9 anti-correlated dual pairs, and identified all 6 phase layers. A \emph{code swap} experiment confirmed full causal control: decoding any digit's image with another digit's 32-bit code produces the target digit's reconstruction with 100\% accuracy ($n=10\times10$ swaps). The discrete code fully determines the output.
    \item \textbf{Pythia-70M Sparse Autoencoder} (EleutherAI \texttt{sae-pythia-70m-32k}, layer~3 MLP, 60 concepts across 6 semantic domains, 12 training checkpoints from step~1 to step~143{,}000): reptimeline tracked feature lifecycle across the full Pythia training trajectory, discovering 34 anti-correlated dual pairs and 3 dependency chains. An \emph{SAE causal intervention} experiment tested all 16 active features by zeroing each in the SAE latent space, decoding back to hidden-state space, and injecting the modified state via forward hook. All \textbf{16/16} features showed \textbf{selective causal effects}: 8 were perfectly selective (affecting only their labeled concepts), and 8 showed finite selectivity averaging $26.8\times$ (best: $98.4\times$ for ``fast''; ``teacher'' at $26.6\times$ disproportionately affected \emph{child, dog, wolf, queen}). Figure~\ref{fig:sae_causal} shows the selectivity heatmap.
\end{itemize}

\noindent These results demonstrate that reptimeline's discovered features are not statistical artifacts but \emph{causally meaningful} primitives: identifiable, labelable, trackable across training, and verifiable via intervention---across architectures ranging from supervised autoencoders to unsupervised language model internals.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our results have several limitations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Small training corpus.} TinyStories (50K stories) limits the language model's semantic sophistication. Scaling to larger corpora (Wikipedia, BookCorpus) would likely improve both language quality and triadic metrics.
    \item \textbf{Token-level projections.} Each token receives an independent projection. With isolated tokens, domain separation ratio is $\sim$1.02 (indistinguishable from noise). However, sentence-level mean-pooling resolves this---see below.
    \item \textbf{Subsumption--language tradeoff at scale (resolved).} With the original $\tanh$ activation, subsumption training at XL scale incurred a +47\% perplexity cost. The iFSQ activation (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}) resolves this: language loss is preserved (0.951 vs.\ baseline 0.946) while achieving 86.5\% held-out subsumption. The remaining gap between 86.5\% and 100\% may yield to longer training or curriculum strategies.
    \item \textbf{Single GPU, limited multi-seed.} Most results are from single training runs. A three-seed validation at XL scale (gap $+0.038 \pm 0.005$, 95\% CI $[+0.029, +0.046]$, 100\% analogy across all seeds) confirms that the core semantic ordering finding is reproducible, but extending multi-seed validation to the full scaling study and transfer experiments would further strengthen the claims.
    \item \textbf{Dead bits (reinterpreted).} Per-bit entropy analysis reveals that $\sim$15 of the 64 bits have low activation probability ($q_i < 0.1$). Figure~\ref{fig:bit_entropy} visualizes the entropy distribution. With the ternary representation (Section~\ref{sec:ternary}), these ``dead'' bits are reinterpreted as the zero state (``irrelevant''), paralleling BitNet b1.58's $\sim$42\% weight zeros. The ternary model's 73\% zero rate is not pathological but reflects the dominance of semantic irrelevance: most primitives are simply not applicable to most concepts.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The loss--embedding interaction.}
Perhaps the most practically important finding is that the optimal alignment loss depends on the quality of the embedding teacher (Section~\ref{sec:transfer}). InfoNCE mines positive/negative pairs from the embedding space, which requires clear cluster structure; MSE matches absolute similarity values, which requires only local pairwise relationships. This interaction is reminiscent of curriculum effects in contrastive learning~\citep{oord2018cpc}: contrastive objectives succeed when the feature space provides meaningful contrast, but degrade when all examples are approximately equidistant.

The loss--embedding interaction suggests a \emph{staged training} strategy: begin with MSE alignment during early training (when embeddings are weak), then switch to InfoNCE once embeddings have matured. We tested this hypothesis (Run~29: MSE for 25K steps, then InfoNCE for 25K steps). The staged model preserves correct semantic ordering (K$\leftrightarrow$Q 66\% $>$ K$\leftrightarrow$D 58\%) and achieves the best perplexity of all triadic runs (7.39), but with a \emph{weaker} semantic gap than pure MSE (+7.8pt vs.\ +29pt). This confirms that the interaction is a property of the embedding \emph{space structure}, not the training stage: even after 25K steps, from-scratch TinyStories embeddings lack the global clustering that InfoNCE requires.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Several directions emerge naturally:
(1)~\emph{Scale--algebra tradeoff (partially resolved)}: D-A17 (355M) achieved only 1.7\% subsumption, but D-A19 demonstrated this was caused by training bugs (alignment-only loss, non-differentiable subsumption), recovering 76.9\% subsumption and 100\% analogy at 355M scale. The remaining gap to D-A14's 98.3\% (40M) may yield to sparsity-preserving training regimes that maintain the $\sim$42\% zero rate at scale.
(2)~Combining the iFSQ activation with richer embeddings (GPT-2 transfer + InfoNCE), potentially achieving both high subsumption and high semantic gap in a single model.
(3)~Exploiting the compositional substrate for multi-step reasoning chains, building on the sub-linear error accumulation demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:composition}.
(4)~Category-aware discrete Rule of Three formulas, which achieve 90.3\% accuracy in ternary space (outperforming continuous arithmetic at 89.9\%), suggesting that fully discrete analogical reasoning is feasible.
(5)~Expanding the discovery loop: 50 hand-labeled anchors yielded 87\% accuracy; 158 anchors yielded 93\%. Automating the discover--label--retrain cycle via reptimeline (using BitDiscovery to propose new anchors, AutoLabeler for semantic grounding, and Reconciler for bidirectional error correction) could scale the anchor set to 300+ and test whether accuracy continues to improve. Cross-backend validation on MNIST and Pythia-70M SAE (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) confirms reptimeline's backend-agnostic operation; a companion paper will provide extended analysis and additional backends.
(6)~Cross-linguistic validation of the ontological primitives across typologically diverse languages.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have presented \microgpt{}, a generative language model that jointly learns next-token prediction and discrete prime-factor semantic representations. Through 29+ training runs spanning architecture search, ablation, scaling studies, bit-width analysis, transfer learning, subsumption recovery, and compositional analysis, we establish eight core results:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The triadic projection head adds \textbf{negligible cost} to language modeling quality ($+1.7\%$ perplexity), acting as a near-free semantic annotation layer.
    \item Meaningful semantic ordering in prime-factor space is an \textbf{emergent property of scale}, crossing zero around $\sim$20M parameters in a gradual crossover. Multi-seed validation confirms reproducibility (gap $+0.038 \pm 0.005$, $n=3$).
    \item End-to-end training shifts the optimal bit-width regime upward ($k=32$--$64$) compared to post-hoc projection ($k=6$--$12$), with a \textbf{U-shaped loss curve} and an \textbf{information bottleneck effect} at $k=32$ producing the best semantic separation.
    \item The alignment loss formulation is the \textbf{primary bottleneck}: replacing MSE with InfoNCE on GPT-2 transfer achieves semantic gap $+0.076$ (closing 48\% of the gap to post-hoc Engine PCA) with perfect analogy verification (100\%), while a \textbf{loss--embedding interaction} dictates that InfoNCE is optimal for rich embeddings and MSE for weak ones.
    \item A differentiable subsumption loss recovers \textbf{near-perfect algebraic subsumption at $k=64$}---98.3\% on 158 held-out pairs (D-A14, 40M params)---resolving the primary limitation of both end-to-end and post-hoc methods at high bit counts, while inducing an \textbf{emergent information hierarchy} where abstract concepts autonomously converge to sparse representations. An initial scaling attempt (D-A17, 355M params) appeared to destroy algebraic structure (1.7\% subsumption), but a corrected experiment (D-A19) using the full four-component triadic loss and differentiable subsumption recovered 76.9\% subsumption and 100\% analogy verification at 355M scale---demonstrating that \textbf{training loss, not model scale, determines algebraic capability}.
    \item The \textbf{iFSQ activation resolves the subsumption--language tradeoff}: replacing $\tanh$ with scaled sigmoid ($2\sigma(1.6x)-1$) preserves language quality (loss 0.924--0.951 vs.\ baseline 0.946) while achieving up to 87.1\% held-out subsumption---compared to the $+47\%$ perplexity cost under $\tanh$. The ternary model produces a clean three-state distribution (1.3\%/73.3\%/25.3\%) that parallels BitNet b1.58's independently discovered sparsity pattern.
    \item The triadic bit space functions as a \textbf{computational substrate}: round-trip accuracy (98.1\%) far exceeds the multiplicative prediction (81.9\%), and two-step transitive chains show sub-linear error accumulation ($+4.5$~pp above prediction). Fork analysis reveals that the mechanism is categorically ontological, not vectorial---unlike word2vec's parallelogram model.
    \item The \textbf{discovery loop scales knowledge}: expanding from 50 hand-labeled anchors to 158 (through bit-semantic analysis, human validation, and retraining) improves holdout accuracy from 87\% to 93\% and subsumption from 90.7\% to 98.3\%. This train--discover--correct--retrain cycle is the mechanism by which the system bootstraps semantic knowledge beyond its initial supervision.
\end{enumerate}

These findings suggest that algebraically verifiable semantic representations are not merely a post-hoc convenience but can be \emph{native} to a generative language model---produced as a natural byproduct of language understanding, at no additional cost. The iFSQ activation resolves the subsumption--language tradeoff that was the system's primary practical limitation. The compositional analysis demonstrates that the bit space supports multi-step algebraic operations with coherent error structure, providing evidence that the representation is not just an encoding but a substrate for reasoning. The scaling experiments (D-A17, D-A18, D-A19) reveal that algebraic structure depends on the \emph{training loss pipeline}---full four-component triadic loss plus differentiable subsumption---not on model size, hybrid partitioning, or ternary sparsity per se; D-A19 recovers 76.9\% subsumption at 355M scale with only 1.0\% quantized zeros, demonstrating that the $\sim$42\% zero rate is optimal but not necessary for algebraic capability. Finally, the anchor expansion cycle (50 $\to$ 158 hand-labeled anchors, 87\% $\to$ 93\% accuracy) provides initial evidence that the discovery loop---train, discover bit semantics, correct, expand anchors, retrain---is the mechanism by which the system scales its knowledge. Together, these results establish bitwise prime-factor semantics as a practical, compositional, and scalable representation within end-to-end generative models.
